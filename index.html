‘There is a broad problem’: Working group recommends big changes to address power-based harassment

By Isabella Ramírez and Zachary Schermele

September 9, 2022

Photo by Rommel Nunez

After years of calls for change, a report released in the spring and unanimously endorsed by a group of faculty, researchers, postdoctoral fellows, students, and administrators from across the University provides a series of bold new solutions to the workplace bullying that has long plagued Columbia and academia writ large.

The anti-bullying working group, co-chaired by Executive Vice President for University Life Dennis Mitchell and professor of biomedical engineering Gordana Vunjak-Novakovic, was brought together in September 2021 by Provost Mary Boyce to make recommendations on how the University should address workplace misconduct and abusive behavior that does not fall under Title IX or other current policies on discrimination.

The recommendations, published in May, attempt to establish a University-wide definition of bullying or “abusive conduct.” If adopted, that definition would include any “pattern of unwelcome conduct that a reasonable person would find hostile, offensive, intimidating, disrespectful, degrading or humiliating,” the report outlines. These behaviors may be physical, oral, or written, including calls, texts, emails, and social media posts occurring off-campus or outside of work hours.

In the report, the working group proposes creating an entirely new Office of Conflict Resolution that would serve as a “central hub” for addressing abusive conduct across the University. If approved by the University Senate, the office would maintain a central database of bullying reports, publish annual data on complaints and resolutions, and support efforts to address incidents of bullying.

“We thought about a central place to go to be informed and equipped to deal with these situations in the best possible way,” Kathryn Johnston, an astronomy professor, told Spectator after the report was released in May.

From September until the end of last semester, the working group met on a weekly basis to review material regarding anti-bullying initiatives at peer institutions and surveys about the state of harassment at Columbia. Mitchell told Spectator in May that the group met “nonstop”—something he said he was proud of.

“This process was spectacular,” he said. “I think it’s important to highlight that because that’s not always the case.”

Since 2015, when conversations about unionization first began among Columbia postdoctoral fellows, the question of bullying was a “central issue” according to Cora Bergantinos-Crespo, Columbia Postdoctoral Workers-United Auto Workers president and a postdoctoral research scientist in genetics and development.

“Since then, until today, we’ve talked to tons of postdocs about their experiences at Columbia and elsewhere,” Crespo said. “In most cases, because of the lack of policy, they leave.”

A 2021 survey, which was cited in the working group’s recommendations, compiled data about power-based harassment experienced by postdoctoral and associate research fellows, scientists, and scholars. It found that over two-thirds of respondents reported experiencing at least one form of power-based harassment, such as belittling, micromanaging, and ignoring concerns.

Of those who reported experiencing power-based harassment, almost half indicated that the behavior had a detrimental effect on their psychological well-being. Respondents 35 and older, associate research scientists, and women were among some of the groups that reported power-based harassment at greater proportions.

The survey identified power-based harassment as “one of the major drivers” of establishing the union, which was officially formed in 2018. CPW-UAW reached its first tentative agreement with the University on June 30, 2020, and one of the major tenets of that contract was what the union called an “unprecedented University commitment [to] work with us to develop policies and procedures to address bullying.”

Rather than adding a specific policy to provide recourse to victims of power-based harassment, however, the University proposed forming a working group that could come up with recommendations to address those types of complaints.

Tulsi Patel, a postdoctoral research fellow in the department of pathology and cellular biology and member of CPW-UAW’s bargaining committee who served on the working group, said the University seemed to be dragging its feet on forming the group at first.

“We started off being a little bit frustrated and skeptical that everything was taking too long to get going,” Patel said. “Once it did get started, from the very beginning it was a very productive collaboration with everyone in the committee, which was really pleasantly surprising.”

To prevent and respond to instances of bullying similar to those described in CPW-UAW’s report, the working group report outlines three key recommendations for the University: define and prohibit abusive conduct, adopt a remedial framework, and promote an anti-bullying culture and climate.

The working group’s first recommendation is for the University to establish a Standard of Professional Conduct explicitly prohibiting bullying and power-based abuse, citing similar policies at the University of California, Berkeley and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. For determining what constitutes abuse, the group suggests considering frequency, severity, power differentials, and context. Not engaging in social niceties, being assertive, and giving constructive criticism do not fall under bullying, according to the working group’s definition.

For remediation, the proposed Office of Conflict Resolution would handle any formal investigations into bullying complaints and provide resources for all involved parties.

Patel said recent anti-bullying agreements at the University of California and Harvard University have a lot of the same themes as the working group’s recommendations.

“It seems clear that there is a broad problem, and everyone is thinking about the solution in similar ways,” she said.

The last of the working group’s recommendations focuses on the prevention of abusive behavior and promoting an anti-bullying culture. One proposed step includes the creation of an anti-bullying advisory committee, separate from the Office of Conflict Resolution, which would assess University progress, suggest policy amendments, and continue to explore the recommendations set forth by the working group. Other courses of action include emphasizing restorative justice, ongoing surveys and focus groups, and mentorship training.

The recommendations were presented to the University Senate at its last meeting before the summer. Boyce wrote in a May press release she anticipates continued “robust discussion” about the report and looks forward to further University community engagement with anti-bullying.

“We thought a lot about what can be done and should be done to change the culture at Columbia so that we prevent this from happening,” Johnston said.
